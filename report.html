Five Key Steps For Making Your Culture More Customer-Centric

Beginner Level: Culture Practices For CX Transformation

WHEN IT COMES TO CX COMPETENCIES, CULTURE IS FIRST AMONG EQUALS

If a company's CX transformation leaders can pick only one

A domain of expertise needed for delivering the intended experiences to customers.

Forrester GlossaryCX competency to master in order to achieve a high-quality customer experience that sticks, they should pick culture. Forrester defines the culture competency as:

Creating a system of shared values and behaviors that focus employees on delivering great customer experiences.

To develop your culture, you need to establish two essential activities that will help you create that system of shared values and guide employees to adopt customer-centric behaviors:

  • Educate employees about your customers, your CX vision, and the employees' roles in fulfilling your vision.

  • Reinforce customer-centric behaviors through routines, celebrations, and rewards aligned to your CX metrics.

For proof of culture's preeminence, look at the companies that are overall leaders in Forrester's Customer Experience Index (CX Index™). (see endnote 1) Firms including Edward Jones, Navy Federal Credit Union, USAA, and Zappos.com do not excel at all six CX competencies, but they do have customer-centric cultures.

Drill down into Forrester's CX Index industry leaders and the pattern holds. Whether it's JetBlue among airlines or Kaiser Permanente among health insurance providers, the top brands in their industries have cultures that obsess about serving customers. These customer-centric cultures matter because they cause companies to deliver great experiences by default and resist forces that might degrade the experience. What's more, because transforming a culture is so difficult, firms that do manage to transform their cultures will enjoy a competitive advantage for years.

SUCCESSFUL CULTURE TRANSFORMATIONS START WITH THESE FIVE STEPS

It's rare for executives to go on record to say that treating customers well is the wrong thing to do. (see endnote 2) Most think that customer centricity is both common sense and something they already do. (see endnote 3) But in reality, as Forrester's CX Index data show, most of these brands fail to deliver experiences that customers value. (see endnote 4) Successful culture transformations clear through the fog by starting with five essential steps that make a compelling case for change, and provide guidance for how to be customer-centric in ways that customers will appreciate. In the first year of a successful culture transformation, companies must:

  1. 1. Develop a clear why for the culture transformation. A crisp statement of purpose for the culture transformation is an insurance policy against the inevitable setbacks that every

    A system of shared values and behaviors that focus employees on delivering great customer experiences.

    Forrester Glossarycustomer-centric culture transformation encounters. The why must draw a causal connection between culture change and business success.

  2. 2. Secure executive support for leading the transformation. Every successful culture transformation Forrester has studied had the full support of senior executives. If you're a CX professional, start at the top, with the CEO. If you're in the C-suite but a lone voice of advocacy for culture transformation, bring your peers onboard first.

  3. 3. Identify a set of shared values. Absent clear guidance, employees will interpret customer centricity in their own way, leading to many conflicting definitions. The shared values must be high-level enough to apply across the organization and be socialized by senior executives. Base these values on the observed behavior of employees delivering great experiences today.

  4. 4. Recognize and reward employees' CX success stories. In the early days of a culture transformation, catching employees in the act of doing the right things is a powerful force for change. It highlights that the organization already has bright spots to build on. It also shrinks the size of the change in the minds of employees when they see that their own behaviors, or those of their peers, are already customer-centric.

  5. 5. Select key performance indicators of progress. Successful transformations take at least four to five years. Organizations need metrics to track their progress. Existing customer and employee metrics like NPS or employee engagement are not the best fit. (see endnote 5) They are lagging indicators of progress. Organizations need metrics that are both leading indicators of change and more fine-grained than benchmark relationship surveys.

Step 1: Develop A Clear Why For The Culture Transformation

A compelling statement of purpose for culture change provides motivation for maintaining the transformation path, even in the face of barriers or setbacks. The why must also connect the culture transformation with clear business benefits. Otherwise, culture change will lose out to competing priorities that do have clear economic benefits. To develop a clear, compelling why, CX leaders must:

  • Explain how transformation success translates to business success. In a 2016 analysis of corporate value statements, we found that treating customers well was the most common statement made by companies (see Figure 1). (see endnote 6) The problem is that many firms don't know how keeping this promise leads to business success. The why must connect the dots between culture change and business impact. Cleveland Clinic's leaders realized that achieving higher patient experience scores would help it maintain its status as a healthcare provider of choice for premium customers around the world. It would also reduce the risk of lawsuits and lead to higher reimbursement rates from the government. Companies with franchise models, like Hilton and Popeyes, see better client experience as enabling their continued growth (see Figure 2). It makes their brands more appealing to franchisees deciding which type of hotel or restaurant to open.

  • Connect the why to both the current state and the intended future state. The why must address how customer-centric the culture is and how transforming the culture aligns with the organization's values. Consider the whys for two firms that have completed successful culture transformations. VCA Animal Hospitals attracts employees who love animals and are passionate about caring for them. The why for its culture change focused on better supporting its passionate employees to deliver the right experience and drive

    A win-win relationship in which engaged consumers reward brands with increased wallet share and word-of-mouth recommendations.

    Forrester Glossarycustomer loyalty. When Cleveland Clinic embarked on its transformation, it was losing patients to other hospitals because it had a reputation for lacking empathy. Its why highlighted the importance of expanding the organization's definition of excellence beyond outcomes to include patient experience and therefore attract more customers.

Step 2: Secure Executive Support To Lead The Transformation

If you don't have executive support for a transformation, don't even bother trying to change the culture. Every successful culture transformation we've studied had the committed support of senior leadership. To get the executive suite fully onboard:

  • Guide leaders to internalize the CX transformation purpose as their own. Long-term support from executives for the culture transformation depends on their developing internal belief in its importance. To spark intrinsic connections, companies like GM, Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE), Popeyes, and Saskatchewan Government Insurance (SGI) have asked executives to tell the culture transformation story in their own words. Popeyes created 34 values cards that aligned with its overall purpose, and it then asked executives to pick the three that resonated most with them. This flexibility enabled executives to craft a personal purpose story, while also ensuring that the personal stories aligned with the company narrative. New South Wales Government Office of State Revenue in Australia holds weekly "huddles" with executives to go through customer feedback. Executives also take ownership of a customer's comment given to staff outside their business area and address it personally — which may involve calling the customer or visiting him or her in person.

  • Nominate one executive to be the visible leader of the transformation. This role is almost always best filled by the CEO. Toby Cosgrove, former president and CEO of Cleveland Clinic, was the visible face of the hospital's transformation. He has told the story of his patient experience epiphany many times: The daughter of a physician who had decided not to go to Cleveland Clinic for surgery, because it was known for a lack of empathy, asked Cosgrove why the hospital didn't teach empathy. This narrative serves as a consistent reminder of why the hospital started on a transformation path. What do you do if leaders haven't had an epiphany yet? Guide them toward the light. In 2011, three midlevel executives at Mercedes Benz USA created a culture training program called Driven To Lead (listen, empathize, add value, delight) to address the discrepancy between the automaker's great cars and so-so service. The program's positive impact helped convince then CEO Steve Cannon that he could lead an even more ambitious customer-centric transformation. (see endnote 7)

Step 3: Identify A Set Of Shared Values

Every organization we interviewed for this research developed a CX vision early in its transformation efforts. To make that vision tangible, CX transformation leaders should create a set of shared values that flow from the vision and clarify the intended experience:

  • Use customer data and employee observation to identify customer-centric behaviors. Examples of existing good experiences are the starting point for creating an appropriate set of CX values for your company. The stories and data that come from observing employees interacting with customers ground these values in real evidence. Farm Credit Services of America (FCS America) used customer feedback to identify which branch offices provided the best customer experience. It then conducted extensive observation of employees, as well as customer and employee interviews, at both high-performing and average-performing locations to identify behaviors that stood out for top performers — an approach known as bright spot analysis (see Figure 3). FCS America used the collected evidence of specific behaviors, like making eye contact, using the client's name, and proactively calling the customer with the status of a loan, as the basis for formulating its values.

  • Document and share the values. Companies must make explicit statements of what a good experience is for their customers. The clarity of these statements helps employees adjust their own behaviors. FCS America developed six CX principles, as well as a customer commitment statement, that apply across the entire organization. VCA did something similar, detailing three emotions customers wanted to feel (valued, connected, and affirmed) and nine client "I wants" that characterized good experiences (see Figure 4). SGI created a set of parallel client and employee promises to highlight that the insurer would look after employees in the same way that it wanted them to look after customers (see Figure 5).

Step 4: Recognize And Reward Employees' CX Success Stories

In our years of studying culture, we've found that most employees believe they are already customer-centric. In general, they do seem to intend to do the right thing. But what is the "right thing" that they should do? To provide clarity, companies must:

  • Celebrate employee behaviors rather than customer outcomes. Recognizing employees and partners solely for getting a high rating from customers is a trap. Instead, recognize employees for specific customer-centric behaviors (see Figure 6). The difference between outcomes — measured by customer ratings — and behaviors is critical. Valuing behaviors helps employees focus on improving aspects of the experience they can control. Valuing outcomes almost always leads to employees finding ways to game the system — like coaching customers on what scores to give or arguing that bad scores were unfair. Companies like Guitar Center, Porsche, and Wells Fargo have found this out the hard way. (see endnote 8)

  • Encourage a culture of recognition. If executives and CX teams are the only ones celebrating customer-centric behaviors, the impact of recognition will be modest. Recognition of customer-centric behaviors must become a common practice across the organization. This is more likely when the recognition connects to processes already in use by employees. In a collaboration with its survey provider, Crowe built its Pay It Forward program on top of its existing client experience survey process. Now, employees mentioned by name in client surveys can highlight which other colleagues, particularly ones behind-the-scenes, contributed to the good client experience. In the year after Crowe implemented Pay It Forward, 50% more employees were recognized for their efforts in delivering an exceptional client experience.

Step 5: Select Key Performance Indicators Of Progress

The final step to establish a culture transformation is determining how to measure progress. Companies need indicators of change that are leading, not lagging, and fine-grained enough that they can detect subtle shifts in behaviors. To choose the right indicators:

  • Track delivery of programs designed to change behaviors. By tracking activities intended to help change behaviors, companies can gauge the impact of training, recognition, and other culture change initiatives. For example, JetBlue and VCA both tracked which employees had gone through customer-centric culture training. This enabled JetBlue to identify a statistically significant difference in NPS for flights with two or more flight attendants who had gone through the training. VCA saw that locations where employees were first trained in its client experience promises were also the first ones to see their client scores go up.

  • Track changes in actual behaviors. There are several ways to track evidence of employee behavior change (see Figure 7). First, companies can survey employees and use the self-reported data to track shifting sentiment or belief. Both Crowe and SGI, for example, saw double-digit increases on employee survey questions related to customer-centric behaviors. Alternatively, companies can add questions to their customer surveys that pertain to specific employee behaviors, or they can simply ask customers to share stories of how employees provided exceptional service (see Figure 8). Some companies use techniques like mystery shopping to observe and validate whether employees are performing the right behaviors. And others have integrated customer-centric behaviors into performance evaluations.

RECOMMENDATIONS

Manage Organizational Expectations For The Pace Of Culture Change

Culture transformations are hard, and they take a long time. Companies will find success more likely if they set reasonable expectations for the difficult road ahead and the pace of change. To do that, CX leaders should:

  • Analogize organizational culture change to individual behavior change. While the challenge of culture change is abstract and hard to internalize for individuals, behavior change is not. Culture changes are composed of a collection of individuals changing their behaviors. Employees can identify with the difficulty of personal behavior change. In our lives, we have all tried to shed bad behaviors like smoking or a poor diet and adopt good behaviors like reading or regular exercise.

  • Reinforce that the transformation will take at least five years. Make sure that all employees, especially executives, know that they are signing up for a long-term project. Customize the timeline in this report to create your own version of a high-level road map that highlights major milestones of the transformation. Be transparent about which metrics you will use to gauge the success of the transformation and what degree of improvement you're looking for in those metrics. For example, SGI asked two questions of employees to gauge their awareness and belief in the insurer's customer promises: 1) Do you understand our customer promises? and 2) Do you think we're ready to deliver on these promises? SGI set a threshold of 90% of employees giving a top-two box rating on both questions before it would share its customer promises publicly.

  • Cite examples of companies that have transformed their cultures. Finally, provide hope to your colleagues that achieving customer centricity is possible. Companies like Cleveland Clinic, Crowe, FCS America, John Deere, Safelite AutoGlass, and VCA have transformed their cultures. Their examples push back against skeptics who point to companies like Southwest, USAA, or Zappos and assert that if you don't start out a customer-obsessed organization there's no hope. Transforming organizational culture to be customer-centric isn't easy, but that just means that transformation success provides a defensible competitive advantage for a long time.
